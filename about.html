Folio III  ·  About the Foundation  ·  Established MMXXVI

The Institution
and Its Mandate.

A standing institution for the deliberate work of partnership between the United States and the nations of the African continent.

§ I   Mandate

What we are.

The U.S.–Africa Allies Foundation is a non-partisan, non-profit institution headquartered in Washington, D.C., chartered to convene American and African leaders around the questions of mutual consequence that the next quarter-century will not allow either side to defer.

We are not an advocacy organization. We are not an aid agency. We are not the diplomatic arm of any government, party, or commercial interest. We are an institution of convening, of policy, and of the patient bridge-building that consequential partnerships require.

We work in five sustained program areas — diplomatic convening, policy and research, economic bridges, the diaspora council, and next-generation leadership — and we expect each of them to outlast any single administration on either continent.

§ II   Method

How we work.

Four operating principles that govern every program we run.

  1. Reciprocity, not benevolence

    The partnership we are building is owed in both directions. We do not run programs whose framing reduces African nations to recipients of American attention.

  2. Proximity, not summitry

    Conferences end when the lights come down. We design for sustained relationships across years — fellowships, recurring convenings, joint working groups — because the work that matters needs time to compound.

  3. Open record, citable work

    Every brief, every joint statement, every piece of policy work we commission is published openly under our seal — readable by working press and citable by working governments.

  4. Generational, not cyclical

    The institutions that endure in American civic life are the ones designed to outlast the cycles that produced them. We mean to be one of them.

§ IV   In Brief

A few articles of conviction.

That the U.S.–Africa relationship deserves the kind of permanent institutional attention that the U.S.–European and U.S.–Asia relationships have long received.

That the African continent is not a problem to be managed but a partner to be engaged.

That the African diaspora in the United States is among the most underused civic constituencies in American public life, and that the Foundation will give it deliberate organizational form.

That the next generation of American and African leaders deserves to meet each other early, and often, and on serious terms.
