§ I   Preamble

The case for a deliberate alliance.

For too long the relationship between the United States and the nations of Africa has been arranged in episodes — a summit here, a bilateral there, an aid package answering a crisis already underway. The continent that will, by mid-century, hold one in four of the world's working-age people deserves a more intentional partner; the United States, in turn, deserves a coherent strategy for the half of the world's growth not yet built.

The U.S.–Africa Allies Foundation exists to close that distance. We are an institution of convening, of policy, of the patient diplomatic work that statesmen of an earlier century would have recognized — adjusted for an era in which the most consequential conversations now happen in Lagos, Nairobi, Dakar, Kigali, and Addis Ababa as readily as in Washington.

Our compact is plain. We will gather American and African leaders in serious rooms, on serious terms, around the questions that determine the next quarter-century: shared security architecture, infrastructure that travels both directions, the diaspora as constitutional bridge, technology cooperation that does not extract, and the climate work no continent can decline.

❦

— The Founders, Washington · 2026

§ II   The Pillars

Five lines of sustained work.

Each program area is a standing commitment, not a campaign. Read in sequence; each entry presumes the one before.

  1. Diplomatic Convening

    Formal and back-channel gatherings of American and African principals — heads of state, legislators, ministers, ambassadors — convened around named questions of mutual consequence.

  2. Policy & Research

    Long-form briefs, white papers, and joint statements developed with university partners on both continents. Published openly. Cited by working governments.

  3. Economic Bridges

    Trade missions, investment forums, and infrastructure dialogues that move capital in both directions and treat African markets as principal — not destination.

  4. Diaspora Council

    The African diaspora in the United States is a constitutional bridge. We organize its civic, professional, and political weight around the partnership the moment requires.

  5. Next-Generation Leadership

    Fellowships, scholarships, and exchanges that place rising American and African leaders in deliberate proximity — early, and for long enough that it matters.

§ III   From the Founder

A letter, in lieu of an introduction.

I built this foundation because the relationship between my two homes — the United States, where I have made my professional life, and the African continent that formed me — has been carried by goodwill rather than design.

Goodwill is not a strategy. The work ahead asks us to be more deliberate, and more honest, than the last quarter-century allowed.
